So what is AI, really?

At its core, AI is pattern recognition — trained on massive amounts of text from the internet.

It reads your prompt, recognizes patterns from its training, and generates the most likely helpful response.

It's not thinking. It's not searching Google in real time. It's drawing on patterns it learned during training.

Why this matters

The way you frame your question shapes which patterns the AI draws from.

Ask casually → get casual patterns.
Ask professionally → get professional patterns.

Different AI companies train on different data with different priorities — which is why the same question can get different answers across tools.

How to Get Great Results

The #1 skill: give AI the full picture of your problem — then let it work

❌ Don't do this

"Write me a service agreement"

Vague prompt = vague output. The AI has nothing to work with, so it guesses.

✓ Do this instead

"I run an Irrigreen irrigation company in Minneapolis. I need a service agreement for residential installations that covers warranty, winterization responsibilities, and payment terms. My typical install is $3,500–$7,000. Keep it professional but not overly legal."

Context + specifics = output you can actually use.

The Master Plumber Problem

Imagine you hire a master plumber from Europe. He's been doing this for 30 years — he knows every pipe, fitting, and code in the book.

But then the American manager walks in and tells him exactly how to do it the American way — step by step, tool by tool.

That master plumber is now working at 10% capacity. The manager assumes he's incompetent. But the real problem? The manager is constraining the expert.

AI works the same way

Tell it the problem, not the method.

Give it the context — what you're building, who it's for, what good looks like. Then let the AI figure out the best way to get there.

Over-instructing leads to worse results. AI often knows methods you don't — let it use them.

Trust, but Verify

AI can be confidently wrong. It's called a hallucination — the output looks right but contains errors. Here's how to catch them:

  • Numbers & pricing — Always double-check any calculations, costs, or measurements against your actual data
  • Specific claims — If AI says "Irrigreen recommends X," verify it with Irrigreen's actual docs
  • Legal language — AI-generated contracts are a starting point, not a finished product. Have your attorney review anything binding
  • Technical specs — Cross-reference installation details with manufacturer specs

Think of AI like a really fast first draft. The value isn't that it's always perfect — it's that it gets you 80% of the way in 5% of the time.

What to Do After This Call

  • Pick one AI tool — ChatGPT, Claude, or Grok. Start with the free version.
  • Try one task today — Write a customer email, price a job, draft a social post. Something you'd normally spend 20 minutes on.
  • Give it context — Tell it who you are, what you do, and what you need. The more it knows, the better the output.
  • Ask it to teach you — Literally ask: "How else can I use you in my irrigation business?"
  • Spot-check the output — Verify numbers, claims, and anything you'll put in front of a customer.

The Exact Prompt That Built This Widget

I'm uploading a CSV export of all customer transactions by line item from QuickBooks. Step 1 — Filter the data: In the line item category column, filter for only line items that include "sod." Ignore everything else — services, materials, irrigation, etc. Step 2 — Calculate total square footage: For every sod line item, pull the quantity field. That number represents square feet. Add all of those quantities together to get our total square feet of sod installed over the last five years. Step 3 — Get the job count: Count the total number of unique invoices (not line items) that contain a sod entry. That's our completed project count. Save that as a separate number. Step 4 — Create a relatable analogy: Take the total square footage and convert it into an analogy that would make sense to most people — something like NFL football fields, city blocks, or acres. Pick whatever is most impressive and easy to visualize. Step 5 — Build the widget: Create a single-file HTML widget I can paste into my website builder. Requirements: • Works on mobile, tablet, and desktop • Each number (sq ft, job count, analogy) scrolls up from zero when the widget becomes visible on screen • Clean, professional design that fits a landscaping company • Below the numbers, include an SEO-dense paragraph describing our experience — mention Minneapolis, the neighborhoods we serve, and the types of projects we do. This helps our site rank in local search.

That's it — one file upload and one prompt. The AI analyzed the spreadsheet, calculated the numbers, wrote the SEO copy, built the animations, and output a working widget ready to paste into my site.
